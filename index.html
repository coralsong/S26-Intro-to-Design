Deli Workers of NYC


By Tony Chen, Neia Dizon, Mimansaa Rao, Victoria Scholl, Coral Song, Yashvi Sureka, and Pauline Youm

Square Deli

168 8th Ave, New York, NY 10011

Interviewed by Victoria Scholl

For this interview, I spoke with Jimmy, a worker at a local deli in New York City who has lived in the city his whole life. Although he is not the owner, he has been working at the deli for about two years, and the store itself has been there for around eight years. From our conversation, it became clear that while the physical layout of the deli hasn’t changed much over time, small design decisions still impact the experience. For example, Jimmy mentioned that they removed a glass barrier near the counter, which made the space feel more open and improved how customers perceived the store. More importantly, the deli functions as a strong community space. Jimmy explained that he knows most of the regular customers, and there is a sense of familiarity where people come in, talk to each other, and interact not just with the workers but with others in the space. This shows how even a simple environment can foster connection. He also pointed out that creativity plays a role in parts of his job that customers might not immediately notice, like the presentation of smoothies and acai bowls. The way food looks can influence how customers feel and whether they return. Overall, the interview highlighted how small design choices, spatial openness, and human interaction all contribute to the atmosphere and success of a neighborhood deli.

Kips Bay Deli

545 2nd Ave, New York, NY 10016

Interviewed by Mimansaa Rao

This interview was more insightful than I initially expected, particularly because the person I spoke with did not identify as a designer, yet clearly engaged in design thinking. One challenge I encountered was encouraging them to reflect on their own creativity. I had to shift two delis before I found a deli worker at Kipsbay Deli, Vinit (he prefers to call himself Vinny). They hesitated at first, which made me realize that people often use creativity in their work without consciously labeling it as such. I had to reframe my questions in simpler terms, which helped the conversation become more fluid. An unexpected insight was their idea to digitize the deli’s loyalty card system. Currently, customers carry multiple physical cards, which they recognize as inconvenient. Their initiative to develop a digital alternative, using their IT background and AI tools, demonstrates strong problem-solving skills and highlights how design can extend beyond visuals into systems and user experience. At the same time, I observed that certain aspects of design are overlooked, particularly in relation to branding and storytelling. While they suggested improvements like warmer lighting, updated posters, and a more appealing exterior, these ideas are not yet connected to a cohesive identity. They described the deli as a “home” for customers, but this concept is not reflected in the physical environment. Their strength lies in practical, user-centered thinking and an intuitive understanding of customer experience. This interview shifted my research focus toward how non-designers engage in design thinking. Moving forward, I plan to explore service design, informal design practices, and the integration of digital and physical experiences.
