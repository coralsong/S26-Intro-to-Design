Square Deli

168 8th Ave, New York, NY 10011

Interviewed by Victoria Scholl

For this interview, I spoke with Jimmy, a worker at a local deli in New York City who has lived in the city his whole life. Although he is not the owner, he has been working at the deli for about two years, and the store itself has been there for around eight years. From our conversation, it became clear that while the physical layout of the deli hasn’t changed much over time, small design decisions still impact the experience. For example, Jimmy mentioned that they removed a glass barrier near the counter, which made the space feel more open and improved how customers perceived the store. More importantly, the deli functions as a strong community space.

Kips Bay Deli

545 2nd Ave, New York, NY 10016

Interviewed by Yashvi Sureka

This interview was more insightful than I initially expected, particularly because the person I spoke with did not identify as a designer, yet clearly engaged in design thinking. One challenge I encountered was encouraging them to reflect on their own creativity. An unexpected insight was their idea to digitize the deli's loyalty card system, which shows how design can extend beyond visuals into systems and user experience.

Punjabi Deli

114 E 1st St, New York, NY 10009

Interviewed by Neia and Mimansaa

The interview started a bit standoffish, as the owner was hesitant to interview due to his lack of comfort level with English, and he directed us to a customer he was serving to speak for him. However, Mimansaa spoke up in Hindi, guaranteeing a leeway into asking our questions. For the questions, I was able to obtain some information about service design and how the owner utilizes it in his own manner. Although, because of the language barrier, most of my questions were answered through observation. For example, there were numbers on the display case with the food inside, and customers not familiar with the dishes would say which number they wanted to purchase. Ketchup and sauces were placed on the table running across the back wall, presumably for easy access for customers after they've bought food. And, despite the language barrier of some customers not knowing Hindi, and Surendar's broken English, conversations between both were prevalent throughout the 45 minutes we stayed in the store. From a service design perspective, these small details revealed how the deli compensates for communication gaps through intuitive systems. The numbered menu, for instance, acts as a universal interface that minimizes confusion and speeds up transactions, especially in a fast-paced environment. This reflects an understanding of customer needs without requiring explicit feedback. Additionally, the spatial layout of the store, particularly the placement of condiments and the clear separation between ordering and pickup areas, demonstrates a flow that supports quick service. I also noticed how regular customers seemed familiar with both the menu and the ordering process, suggesting that the service design fosters repeat behavior through consistency and ease of use. Even the informal interactions between the owner and customers contributed to a sense of community, which is an intangible but crucial component of service design!

Brooklyn Fare Deli

227 Cherry St, New York, NY 10002

Interviewed by Tony Chen

In this segment from Brooklyn Fare interviews, the neighborhood deli is revealed to be a "living archive" where the design intelligences of the workers are embedded in the everyday operations of the shop. We see how Dominican Manny Acosta, the shop's florist, uses white carnations to signal welcome to Brooklyn Fare's patrons. He maintains a cultural archive of bacalau (dried and salted codfish, popular in many Latin American countries) for the long-time customers of Brooklyn Fare. He also keeps a personal archive of details about individual customers (e.g. Chiqui likes 2% over skim milk) that he records in his mind. His strategy is in direct contrast to that of manager Carmen Luna, who is also a student of interior design. Carmen applies formal visual hierarchies that she is studying in school to the specials board of Brooklyn Fare in order to improve the performance of the shop. Unlike Brooklyn Fare, the deli is not a historical site that has been authored by the community, rather it is a space of community authorship and every design decision made in the shop actively writes the history of the neighborhood.

Stuytown Market Place

279 1st Ave, New York, NY 10003

Interviewed by Coral Song

Jamal, the owner/manager of Stuytown Market Place, has been part of the whole process in the construction of this deli shop. From location setting to menu developments, Jamal has been and is currently still working towards making Stuytown Market Place as friendly for its neighbor customers as possible. Stuytown Market Place was first launched in October of 2023 and has become a steadily growing market, making itself one of the strongest go-to options especially for the New School students residing in Stuyvesant Dorm and 301. Jamal mentions that he wanted the store to become another “home” for students like us, working towards creating a cozy vibe, available 24/7 with various menu options, as well as keeping a clean store. In terms of designing the store and its interiors, Jamal is more of a “let's try first and see where it leads” type of designer. When he has any ideas, he decides to push and try it out first, and determines afterwards whether it was the right choice or not. This courageous decision making has led to multiple successes in running Stuytown Market Place so far. One of Jamal's most proud achievements with the store is with the menu. Not only does Stuytown Market Place have a huge variety in items they offer, but having to recently add acai bowls to the menu has earned them a good fortune through selling more than 100 bowls daily. While everything seems like it naturally fell into place, the time and effort it takes to run and maintain a store like this is some dedication, especially having experience with working at a food-selling store in NYC. I can't imagine the amount of thoughts Jamal thinks about the store every moment of the day.

Neighborhood delis as spaces for design, care, memory, labor, and community.
